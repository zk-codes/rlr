Zines: A History

From DIY Science Fiction Fanzines to Digital Self-Publishing

Introduction

Zines—short for fanzines or magazines—are self-published, small-circulation works produced by individuals or small groups. They exist outside traditional publishing channels and often reflect the passion, creativity, and idiosyncratic views of their creators. Throughout their history, zines have been a voice for the marginalized, a playground for the creative, and a community-building tool for those existing outside mainstream culture. Their DIY ethos has inspired generations of creators and established a rich tradition of independent publishing that continues to evolve in our digital age.1

This article traces the evolution of zines from their humble beginnings in science fiction fan communities through their adoption by various countercultural movements, to their current renaissance in both print and digital forms. By understanding the history of zines, we gain insight into how grassroots publishing has served as a vital outlet for expression outside commercial and institutional constraints.

Sci-Fi Starts It All

The earliest recognizable zines emerged in the 1930s among science fiction fans. As pulp magazines like Amazing Stories gained popularity, readers began connecting through letter columns and fan clubs. This community of enthusiasts wanted more than just passive consumption—they wanted to participate in the conversation about the genre they loved.2 The first true science fiction fanzine, The Comet, was published in 1930 by the Science Correspondence Club in Chicago. This mimeographed publication set the template for what would become a flourishing fan press.

By the 1940s, science fiction fanzines had proliferated. Notable examples included Spaceways, Novae Terrae (later renamed New Worlds), and Le Zombie. These publications featured fan fiction, reviews, essays, correspondence, and artwork. Despite their amateur status, many fanzines maintained impressive production standards and fostered communities that would eventually produce professional writers like Ray Bradbury, Harlan Ellison, and Marion Zimmer Bradley—all of whom got their start in fanzines.3

The production methods of these early zines were labor-intensive. Most were created using mimeograph machines, which required creators to type or draw on stencils that were then attached to a drum filled with ink. As the drum rotated, ink was forced through the stencil onto paper. This technology was relatively affordable and accessible, allowing fans with limited resources to publish their work. Hand-collating, stapling, and mailing these publications was often a social event, bringing fans together for "collating parties."4

Science fiction zines created a participatory culture that expanded beyond just publishing. They announced fan gatherings, conventions, and club meetings, helping to build a nationwide (and eventually international) community of science fiction enthusiasts. The concept of "fandom" as we understand it today largely emerged from this vibrant zine culture, establishing patterns of collaborative creation and community building that would influence future generations of zine-makers.

"Science fiction fandom isn't just a group of people who like the same thing. It's an ongoing conversation, and fanzines were the first places where that conversation happened in a permanent, documentable way."

— Cory Doctorow, author and digital rights activist

Riot! Make A Zine!

While science fiction fans pioneered the zine format, it was the punk movement of the 1970s that truly revolutionized zine culture and expanded its reach. Punk's DIY ethos—the belief that anyone could form a band, organize a show, or publish their thoughts—was perfectly suited to the zine medium. Punk Magazine, founded in 1976 by John Holmstrom and Legs McNeil, helped codify the visual aesthetic and attitude of punk zines: cut-and-paste layouts, ransom note typography, raw photography, and confrontational writing.5

The accessibility of photocopying technology in the 1970s and 1980s dramatically transformed zine production. Unlike mimeograph machines, photocopiers allowed for easier reproduction and greater visual experimentation. Creators could incorporate photographs, detailed illustrations, and complex collages into their work. This technological shift coincided with punk's embrace of amateurism and raw expression, creating a perfect storm for zine proliferation.6

Iconic punk zines like Sniffin' Glue (UK), Slash (Los Angeles), and Maximum Rocknroll (San Francisco) documented local scenes, reviewed records and shows, published interviews, and provided practical information about venues, bands, and underground resources. These publications served not just as media outlets but as community organizing tools, helping to build and sustain punk scenes across North America, Europe, and beyond.

The Riot Grrrl movement of the early 1990s marked another pivotal moment in zine history. Emerging from the punk scenes in Olympia, Washington and Washington D.C., Riot Grrrl combined feminist politics with punk's DIY approach. Zines became central to this movement, with publications like Bikini Kill, Girl Germs, and Jigsaw creating spaces for young women to discuss sexism, abuse, sexuality, and empowerment outside mainstream feminist discourse.7

Riot Grrrl zines often employed intimate, confessional writing styles and challenged conventional ideas about professionalism and objectivity. Personal stories became political statements, and the line between diary and manifesto blurred. The famous Riot Grrrl mantra "Revolution Girl Style Now!" encapsulated the urgency and agency these zines promoted. Distribution often happened hand-to-hand at shows, through the mail, or via feminist bookstores, creating networks of readers and writers across the country.

"I steal my brother's typewriter and I started to write...I want a magazine that pleads for righteousness, the due and rights of woman. I need a magazine that says angry things, a raw guttural voice, an insistent pygmy cry."

— Kathleen Hanna, from Bikini Kill zine #2, 1991

The Current State Of The Zine

The late 1990s and early 2000s saw many predicting the death of print zines as the internet became increasingly accessible. Why go through the labor-intensive process of printing and distributing physical zines when one could more easily reach audiences through websites, blogs, and later social media? Yet contrary to these predictions, print zines have not only survived but experienced a remarkable resurgence in the digital age.8

Today's zine landscape is characterized by diversity in both content and form. Contemporary zines cover virtually every subject imaginable: personal narratives, poetry, politics, art, music, food, health, identity, specialized hobbies, and more. Production values range from deliberately raw, handmade aesthetics to sophisticated designs created with professional software and printed on high-quality paper. Distribution networks have expanded through dedicated zine fests, independent bookstores, specialized distros, and online marketplaces like Etsy.

The digital revolution, rather than killing zines, has created a hybrid publishing ecosystem. E-zines, PDF zines, and web comics adopt zine sensibilities for digital formats. Social media platforms help creators promote physical zines to wider audiences, while online communities like We Make Zines connect creators across geographical boundaries. Many zinesters move fluidly between digital and analog formats, choosing the medium that best suits their particular project.9

Institutional recognition of zines has also grown significantly. Libraries such as the Barnard Zine Collection, the Salford Zine Library (UK), and the Papercut Zine Library in Boston have established dedicated archives preserving zine history. Academic courses on zine-making are now offered at universities, and museums like the Museum of Contemporary Art in Chicago have featured exhibitions on zine culture. This institutional attention has brought new audiences to zines while raising questions about what happens when underground media is preserved within mainstream institutions.10

The COVID-19 pandemic further demonstrated the resilience of zine culture. As people sought creative outlets during lockdowns, many turned to zine-making as a tangible, screen-free activity. Virtual zine fests emerged when in-person events were canceled, and mail-order zine distribution thrived. The pandemic reinforced the value of physical media as a counterbalance to digital fatigue.

"Zines remind us that you can create and distribute media on your own terms. In a world where so much of our interaction happens through profit-driven platforms, the simple act of making and sharing a physical object becomes almost radical."

— Jenna Freedman, Librarian and founder of the Barnard Zine Collection

Toward A Zinester Future

What does the future hold for zines? Rather than being rendered obsolete by digital media, zines appear to be finding new relevance in the 21st century. Several trends suggest directions for the continued evolution of zine culture.11

First, zines increasingly serve as a deliberate analog counterpoint to digital life. In reaction to screen fatigue and algorithm-driven content, many creators and readers are embracing the tactile, finite nature of print zines. The physical object—something you can hold, smell, and experience without notifications or tracking—offers a different kind of engagement than digital media. This "slow media" approach values human-scale communication and intentional consumption over viral reach.

Second, zines continue to provide spaces for marginalized voices and perspectives underrepresented in mainstream publishing. BIPOC creators, LGBTQ+ communities, disabled artists, and others use zines to document their experiences, share resources, and build community outside institutional gatekeeping. Zines about mental health, chronic illness, and neurodiversity have become particularly prevalent in recent years, creating peer support networks through shared stories.12

Third, educational applications of zines are expanding. Teachers from elementary school through university levels are incorporating zine-making into their curricula, recognizing their value for developing critical media literacy, creative expression, and research skills. Librarians have become particularly strong advocates for zines, creating workshops, collections, and programs that introduce new generations to self-publishing. The accessibility of zine-making makes it an ideal entry point for young people to become not just media consumers but creators.13

Fourth, the intersection of digital and analog zine practices continues to evolve in interesting ways. Some creators are exploring augmented reality components that bridge their physical zines with digital content. Others are using blockchain technology to create verifiable limited editions of digital zines. Print-on-demand services have made short-run publishing more accessible, allowing creators to avoid the upfront costs of large print runs while still producing physical objects.

As we look to the future, zines will likely continue their dual role as both anachronistic artifacts and innovative media forms. Their flexible nature allows them to adapt to changing technologies and cultural contexts while maintaining their core values of independence, authenticity, and community building. In an era of media consolidation and platform monopolies, the decentralized, small-scale nature of zine publishing offers an alternative model for how creative work can be produced and shared.14

"Zines aren't just about nostalgia for print. They're about creating spaces where different rules apply—where success isn't measured in clicks or sales, but in meaningful connections and authentic expression. That need doesn't go away with new technology; if anything, it becomes more essential."

— Mimi Thi Nguyen, scholar and zinester

Whether produced on typewriters, photocopiers, or digital printers, whether distributed hand-to-hand or promoted via Instagram, zines remain a powerful reminder that publishing belongs to everyone. The history of zines shows us that when people take media into their own hands, remarkable things happen—communities form, movements grow, and new voices find their way into the world. As long as there are stories to tell and ideas to share outside mainstream channels, the spirit of zine culture will continue to thrive.
